The Ultimate Guide to Buying Laptops in Nairobi.

In Nairobi's bustling tech market, finding a reliable laptop can feel like a gamble. Whether you're looking at an HP EliteBook, a Dell Latitude, or a MacBook Pro, the difference between a great deal and a "lemon" comes down to knowing what to check.

1. The "Ex-UK" Myth vs. Reality

People use the term "Ex-UK" as a mark of quality, but it really just means the laptop was previously used in a corporate environment. Corporate laptops are built for durability (Magnesium alloy chassis, spill-resistant keyboards), which is why we prefer them at Nexcom over brand-new "consumer" laptops that are made of thin plastic.

The Nexcom Quality Checklist

Before you pay a single shilling to any shop (including ours!), perform these tests:

  • The Battery Cycle Test: On MacBooks, check 'System Report'. On Windows, run powercfg /batteryreport. Anything over 80% health is a win.
  • The "White Screen" Test: Open a blank white page. Look for tiny bright spots (dead pixels) or "bruises" on the panel.
  • Keyboard Stress Test: Don't just type your name. Open a notepad and hit EVERY single key.
  • SSD Health: Use a tool like CrystalDiskInfo. If the drive is "Caution," don't buy it.

2. Top Picks for 2026

Based on durability and spare part availability in Kenya right now:

  • Best for Students: Lenovo ThinkPad T480 or T490. Dual battery options and virtually indestructible.
  • Best for Designers: MacBook Pro M1/M2 (Refurbished). The screen color accuracy is unmatched for the price.
  • Best for Heavy Office Work: HP EliteBook 840 G7/G8. Modern look, great port selection, and easy to upgrade RAM.
